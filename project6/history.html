Maps: Who Needs Them?

Home History Maps in Use

Despite not always looking like today’s technologically advanced maps, the art and science of mapmaking is more than 5,000 years old. Early maps mostly focused on small areas such as trading routes, cities, military plans, or hunting areas. These maps were also less frequently oriented with north at the top. Both of these traits are the result of people not traveling very far outside of their home region and not being aware of the planet as a whole. A world map would be of little use to a wheat farmer in Mesopotamia. Maps from this time period include Egyptian maps to record property boundaries, Babylonian sketches on tablets, and silk maps from China.

One of the next biggest turning points in cartographic history (cartography being the practice of making maps) was a collection of maps constructed by geographer Claudius Ptolemaeus of Roman Egypt in 150 A.D. The paper featured a collection of maps from regions all over the world. The lines of latitude and longitude he included “revolutionized European geographic thinking, by imposing mathematical rules to the composition of maps”

In the middle ages, the majority of progress in the field of cartography was made in the Islamic world. An Arab scholar in the court of Sicily made a ton of maps and geographic books. His work was incredibly impactful, his designs influencing Islamic map makers for years to come.

In the European Renaissance, a few key factors dramatically shifted the map making landscape. The combination of a growing public interest in learning and the invention of the printing press, which removed map making from the domination of religious organizations, democratized the field and allowed for greater access. (History of Mapping 2022.)

These days, vast advancements in technology allow for the production of incredibly detailed maps at the touch of a button, but the basic principles and aesthetics for the creation of maps still stand.
